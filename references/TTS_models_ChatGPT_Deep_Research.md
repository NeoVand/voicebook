## What is actually browser-ready with Transformers.js and WebGPU

Transformers.js enables WebGPU acceleration simply by loading a model with `device: 'webgpu'`, and the v4 release adds better operator support and broader architecture coverage than earlier releases. That matters for TTS, because the real gating factor in 2026 is no longer whether a TTS model exists on Hugging Face, but whether the model has a **web-friendly ONNX export** and whether its operators behave well under **ONNX Runtime Web + WebGPU**. ## The models I would choose for your app

If I were building your app today, I would ship **three user-facing quality tiers** rather than pretending one model solves everything.

## How I would architect the product

The technical foundation should be **Transformers.js v4**, not the older v3 stack, because v4ŌĆÖs rewritten WebGPU runtime improves operator coverage and specifically broadens the architecture set you can reasonably target in-browser. The v4 release also introduced a `ModelRegistry` workflow with functions such as `get_pipeline_files`, `get_file_metadata`, `is_pipeline_cached`, `clear_pipeline_cache`, and `get_available_dtypes`, which is exactly what you want for a product that needs to explain download sizes, choose sensible precision defaults, and cache models on-device between sessions. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn29view0Ņłü

At runtime, I would detect **WebGPU availability** first and then choose a model tier accordingly. If WebGPU is available, load Kokoro or Supertonic with `device: 'webgpu'`. If WebGPU is not available, fall back to a smaller WASM-capable model such as Kitten Nano or MMS. That kind of device-aware routing is not a luxury; it is how you keep the app usable across browsers and laptops. The official WebGPU guide for Transformers.js explicitly documents the `device: 'webgpu'` pattern, and the Kitten model cards spell out that some variants must remain WASM-only. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn29view1Ņłéturn27view0Ņłéturn27view1Ņłéturn28view0Ņłü

For the actual **audiobook UX**, I would not synthesize an entire pasted markdown file in one monolithic call, even when the model could theoretically accept a large input. I would parse the markdown into semantic chunksŌĆöheadings, paragraphs, block quotes, list items, and possibly sentence groupsŌĆöthen maintain a synthesis queue that stays a chunk or two ahead of playback. That reduces cancellation pain, keeps memory bounded, allows the user to start listening almost immediately, and makes it easy to export either a single merged file or chapter-level files. This approach also fits the reality that several of the most interesting browser models either rely on speaker reference assets, downloadable style vectors, or context-sensitive generative pipelines that are much easier to manage in chunked form than in ŌĆ£feed the entire book at onceŌĆØ mode. The cited model cards reflect that reality through explicit use of speaker embeddings, downloadable voice bins, or audio prompts rather than a single magical long-context narration API. I would also design the UI around **two kinds of voices**. The first kind is **preset voices**ŌĆöKokoro, KittenTTS, SupertonicŌĆöwhich are perfect for quick-start audiobook reading. The second kind is **reference-driven voices**ŌĆöChatterbox and OuteTTSŌĆöwhich are better framed as advanced features, because they depend on either audio prompts or speaker encodings and need more control surfaces.
